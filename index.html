Takeaways

One of the first books that I read in this course was the story written by Aviaq Johnson “Those Who Run in the Sky.” This was the book that created the depiction of the image above that you initially saw. This book taught me a lot about reverence and how Inuit culture specifically behaves and uses stories to deepen their connections and love. It brought together really highlighted moments of how Indigenous stories have lots of deeper meaning and connections and how not everything is as black and white. It was one of the stories that I read and actually thought was impressive because it showed how Indigenous authors can use storytelling to create actual heartfelt books that people may enjoy reading further, which changed my perspective on how I viewed Indigenous stories and authors! Aside from reverence, I learnt interconnectedness and how everything is all connected to the land, water, and amazing mother nature. I learnt how resilience is brought in and how it's highly valued among Indigenous communities, especially that of the Inuit culture! The book also touched base on clear instances of assimilation done through references that geared indirectly towards European colonization! Growing up in a predominantly settler community, Indigenous stories and perspectives were rarely part of my life experience until taking Contemporary Aboriginal Voices this semester. Through analyzing Aviaq Johnston's profoundly moving novel Those Who Run in the Sky, I gained crucial insights into Inuit epistemologies and came to a deeper understanding of my own role and responsibilities.


<>I was struck by how Johnston's vivid depictions of Inuit spirituality allowed readers to experience ancestral wisdoms from the inside out. By following Pitu's journey of personal growth through facing relentless hardship, I gleaned profound life lessons about resilience, gratitude, and embracing communal responsibilities. Pitu's maturation from a disrespectful youth to a young man of profound reverence holds valuable mirrors for self-reflection. Though his beloved Saima married another, Pitu embodied ancestral teachings by respecting their union rather than indulging in jealousy—a lesson not lost on me regarding respecting relationships. Discussing these insights with my parents and friends revealed gaps in our collective knowledge about Indigenous realities. I realized how easy it can be to drift through life ignorant of oppression's intergenerational impacts right here in our own backyards. This course lit a fire within me to embrace “Reconcili-ACTION” through learning more about our territory's original stewardship nations, championing Indigenous leadership, advocating for clean water, and demanding land back. Going forward, I vow to cultivate reverence by embracing humility, engaging local knowledge keepers respectfully, and embodying responsibilities as a newcomer through concrete allyship and environmental stewardship.


In conclusion, this book made me understand the true value of life and the basic necessities that we take for granted. It also made me open my vision on Indigenous assimilation and historical issues. Previously, I was blinded-sighted and annoyed by the constant talk of it, but now I deeply reflected and even used some of the books' lessons as inspiration to my own learning. I shared many of these stories with my online friends but also my family, showcasing to them how lucky we are not to have to deal with the extremely hard hardships others might have to go through!

Aviaq Johnston's captivating short story “The Haunted Blizzard” illuminated profound lessons about embracing ancestral wisdom and respecting the vulnerabilities of others. Following Panik’s harrowing experience during an ominous storm, I reflected deeply on my responsibilities as an outsider resident on these lands. Johnston skillfully conveyed the rich cultural knowledge held within Indigenous storytelling through Nita’s grandmother’s ominous warnings. Her dire pleas to “stay with us” and avoid isolation reflected a sincere duty to protect youth, exemplifying reverence for community. However, Panik’s mother expressed skepticism, symbolizing eroding respect for ancestral guidance. This juxtaposition underscores intergenerational divides in comprehending local realities.


Witnessing Panik’s sheer terror confronting manifestations of folklore highlighted empathy’s importance. Where adults dismiss “imagination,” children grasp multidimensional truths, as their minds remain open. Elders pass down intangible wisdoms ensuring resilience through adversity. Though uncertainty cloaked descriptions, spiritual teachings centered relational duties, prioritizing safety above individualism. This left a profound impression on cultivating care through uncertainty. Moving forward, I vow to follow Panik’s transforming outlook. Where skepticism once clouded my perspective, embracing humility means engaging Indigenous knowledge respectfully. Locally, this involves attentively listening to knowledge keepers about responsibilities regarding natural sites, seeking permission before accessing territories, and championing language/tradition revitalization.


On a broader scale, it means recognizing oppression’s lasting impacts through generational trauma and advocating for clean waters, language laws, and missing persons inquiries. As an ally, I aim to integrate ancestral teachings respecting all relations into daily lives through plant-based diets, shifting industrial paradigms threatening Mother Earth, whose health sustains all equally. It further means looking not just at Indigenous cultures but society as a whole and realizing when I am being pressured or when I am not, or when my gut delivers that feeling of “stop” or when it doesn’t and so on and so forth. It also means seeing how life and my environment subconsciously affects me, and reflecting on that to improve my own personal skills and influence myself for the better Most importantly, it means cultivating profound reverence within relationships by acknowledging vulnerabilities, acknowledging one’s positionality, and strengthening community ties. Though challenges persist, focusing on interconnectivity between all peoples provides solace. This story powerfully illuminated pathways towards walking respectfully on these lands through reverence, resilience and relational duties.

My Vision for this website

My vision for this artifact was to create an engaging resource that would foster deeper understanding of Indigenous knowledge and lived experiences among learner audiences. Through multimedia storytelling and highlighting transformative themes, I hoped to inspire profound reflection on responsibilities as newcomers to these territories. It was important for me to authentically honor ancestral wisdoms while ensuring a respectful and accessible experience. I want individuals to come away feeling motivated to embrace reconciliation through cultivating empathy, championing sovereignty, building compassionate relations and championing wellness through small thoughtful actions.


In constructing this site, I carefully considered prominent lessons from course works like those symbolized through characters' journeys towards reverence in The Grizzlies film and Johnston's novels. I included location profiles, cultural videos, interactive activities and advocacy resources to holistically demonstrate reconciliation principles in an interactive manner. The clean aesthetic aimed to prioritize messaging while subtly conveying responsibility. Drawing from literature circle discussions, I incorporated diverse understandings around developing care through facing hardship, reconnecting culture and traditions, acknowledging oppression's impacts and nurturing communal belonging across differing perspectives. My goal was an experiential learning tool synthesizing how each individual truly embraces responsibilities as newcomers through relationship-centered living.
